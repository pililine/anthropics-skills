# My Customer Email

## What this skill does

Handles customer-facing email in Chinese and English: writing, rewriting, polishing, and translation.

For general tone and wording preferences, refer to `my-writing-style`. This skill focuses on customer email structure, scenarios, and conventions.

## When to use

- Writing or polishing a new customer email
- Translating a customer email between Chinese and English
- Following up on samples, testing, orders, or project status
- Communicating quotations, pricing, or delivery timelines
- Scheduling or confirming meetings
- Clarifying technical questions or requirements with a customer
- Sending a polite reminder or re-engagement message
- Reconnecting after a holiday or a long gap

## Default email structure

1. Opening — brief acknowledgment or context (one sentence)
2. Main point — state the purpose clearly, early
3. Details — relevant information, numbers, options, or timeline
4. Next step — what you are asking for, or what happens next
5. Polite close

Cut anything that doesn't help the reader understand or act.

## Chinese email

- 先说目的，再补背景。
- 关键时间节点、数量和待确认事项要单独标出，不要埋在长段里。
- 语气礼貌但直接；不要过度客套。
- 结尾不用"如有不妥，敬请见谅"等空话，除非真有歉意。
- 多个要点用短段或列表，不要连成一大段。

## English email

- Subject line should state the purpose clearly (e.g., "Sample Shipment Update – [Product]").
- Open with the point. Skip "I hope this email finds you well" unless this is a reconnect message.
- Keep sentences short and easy to scan.
- Use bullet points when listing two or more items.
- Close with a specific ask or clear next step.

## Common scenarios

**Sample or testing update**
State what was sent or completed, when, and what the next step is. Invite confirmation or questions.

**Quotation**
State what is included, the price, and the validity period. Keep the tone neutral. Do not volunteer cost breakdowns unless the customer asks.

**Meeting scheduling**
Propose specific times. State the purpose briefly. Offer one or two alternatives.

**Technical clarification**
State the specific question clearly. Avoid jargon if the reader is not technical. Offer a follow-up channel (a call or a document to follow).

**Polite reminder or follow-up**
Acknowledge the previous message briefly. Restate what is needed and why it matters. Keep the tone cooperative, not pressuring.

**Post-holiday or re-engagement**
Lead with the business reason for reaching out. Keep it brief and warm. Avoid generic openers like "long time no see."

## Output format

- Provide Subject line and Email body.
- For Chinese: write in Chinese unless I ask for bilingual or English.
- For English: write in English.
- If the appropriate tone is unclear, provide two versions — one more formal, one warmer — labeled clearly.

## Avoid

- Promises or claims not backed by confirmed facts
- Made-up delivery dates, pricing, test results, or specifications
- Overly aggressive or demanding language
- Excessive apologies that weaken credibility
- Real customer names, company names, or confidential internal data
